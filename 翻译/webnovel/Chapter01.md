# Chapter 1: Fifty-Two Thousand

Later, a lot of people asked me: how did you dare buy that place?

I said, it was cheap.

That wasn't the whole truth, though.

Fifty-two thousand. In 2003, fifty-two thousand yuan for a two-bedroom, sixty-square-meter apartment. Sixth floor, no elevator—but it was still two bedrooms and a living room.

I was twenty-four then, working in a small southern city. Sales job, selling hardware fittings. Base salary eight hundred a month, plus commission. Good months I could pull fifteen hundred; bad months I got that eight hundred and nothing else.

The company didn't provide housing. I was renting a single room for three hundred a month. No AC. In summer I'd toss and turn, too hot to sleep. In winter my breath came out white inside the quilt. The toilet was broken; every time I had to flush, I'd fill a basin with water and pour it down.

Food was even tighter. Two buns for breakfast, one yuan. Lunchbox for three fifty. Instant noodles for dinner. I kept monthly living expenses under three hundred. No new clothes, no gatherings, quit smoking—you think I didn't want to? Couldn't afford it.

That's how I saved for two years.

The day I opened the passbook and saw the forty-two thousand on it, I honestly felt like crying. Add the year-end bonus and some loose change, I had a bit over forty-eight thousand.

Back then housing prices in that small city weren't like the big metros, but good locations still ran over two thousand per square meter. Forty-eight thousand wouldn't even buy you a bathroom.

I hit up multiple agents, looked at more than ten places. Some ground floors were damp, the wall paint peeling off in sheets. Some backed right onto a garbage station, windows sealed shut. Some had cracks in the walls and still asked for eighty thousand. None of them worked out.

Then there was this agent, surname Zhou, mid-thirties, skinny, always smiling, the type who acted like your best friend the moment you met him.

He handed me a cigarette by the roadside—I'd quit, but I took it, didn't light it, just tucked it behind my ear.

"Little Qin," he said, "what's your actual budget?"

I told him straight: "Under fifty thousand."

He exhaled smoke, looked at me for a long moment.

"There's one place," he said. "Fifty-two thousand. But…"

That "but" hung there a while.

"But what?"

"It's been empty a long time. Old lady passed away a couple years ago. Kids are all out of town, asked me to help sell it." He stubbed out his cigarette. "You wanna take a look?"

Back then I thought it was normal. Old person dies, kids sell the house, happens all the time.

Brother Zhou took me on his motorcycle. We cut through several streets, going farther and farther out, to an old residential complex in the north of the city. Six-story red brick buildings, the cement exterior flaking, exposing the red brick underneath. A few old Chinese scholar trees below, their shade covering a wide patch, pleasant to walk under even in summer.

I followed him upstairs.

He walked slow, one step at a time. The stairwell was quiet. The motion-sensor lights only came on if you stomped. One floor, light on; next floor, light on; the one behind me went dark. First floor, second, third… Two units per floor, doors shut, the Spring Festival couplets faded, their corners flapping in the wind.

At the sixth floor, Zhou pulled out a key. Unit 601.

He inserted it, turned twice, the door opened. Then he handed me the key and didn't go in himself.

"You go in and look around."

"You're not coming in?"

"I'll wait out here for you." He pulled out another cigarette, lit it, leaned against the wall. "It's a decent-sized place. Take your time, no rush."

I found him odd. Aren't agents supposed to follow you in, talking nonstop? Good orientation, great lighting, school district… This one didn't even step through the door.

Then I thought, maybe the commission's too small to bother with. Fifty-two thousand place, how much commission could there be?

I didn't think much of it and pushed the door open.

The smell hit me right away—not mold, just that old-house smell, dust mixed with aged wood. But strangely, it wasn't strong. It was even faint. A place empty for two years should reek way worse.

The windows were open.

The living room faced south, sunlight streaming straight in, illuminating every dust particle in the air. Two bedrooms, kitchen, bathroom all there. White floor tiles in the living room, already too grey to tell they were ever white. Old floral wallpaper on the walls, edges peeling, curling up with dust.

The furniture was still there. Living room had a set of old leather sofas, dark brown, the leather cracked in several places. The coffee table had a glass top, yellowed newspapers tucked underneath. The TV stand was a low cabinet, a bulky CRT TV still sitting on top, screen coated in dust.

The kitchen was fairly clean. A cast iron wok sat on the stove, lid on. Next to it was an enamel bowl, still holding two chopsticks.

I wondered at the time—why was the wok still covered if she was gone? Later I figured she probably cooked her last meal and didn't get the chance to put it away.

The bedrooms weren't small either. Master faced south, a large wooden bed, a bedside lamp with a dusty shade. The smaller bedroom faced north, a bit tighter, lighting okay, but the window was closed and the stuffy smell was stronger there.

I walked around, and the more I looked, the more satisfied I got.

Old, sure. But fix it up, repaint the walls, swap the flooring, and it was livable. Sixth floor? Sixth floor was fine. Young guy, stairs are exercise.

The key thing was, sixty square meters. Two bedrooms, one for me, rent out the other for three or four hundred a month, easy. Mortgage? None. Fifty-two thousand paid in full. Never paying rent to a landlord again.

I stood in the middle of the living room, already imagining my life here. Big color TV on the wall, a computer desk, a fridge. Kitchen fixed up, cooking for myself on weekends. The balcony was small but enough to hang laundry.

"How is it?" Zhou called from the door.

"Pretty good." I walked out. "Fifty-two thousand, can't you go any lower?"

"Little Qin, I really can't go lower. You know this location, this size, normal price would be seventy or eighty thousand." He stubbed out his cigarette. "If the old lady hadn't passed suddenly and the kids weren't out of town desperate for money, it wouldn't be this price."

I thought about it. He was right. Seventy or eighty thousand versus fifty-two—a gap of nearly half.

"Can I see the property deed?"

"Sure. Come by another day and I'll show you."

I went back two more times. First trip, I saw the deed. Old lady's name, Li Guifang, passed away in 2001. All three children had signed power of attorney, letting Zhou handle everything. The title was clean, no disputes.

Second trip, I signed the contract.

Contract signing was inside that same apartment. The owners didn't come back, had Zhou sign on their behalf. The contract was simple. I read it twice, nothing fishy. Handwritten, clear as day: total price fifty-two thousand, paid in full, transfer fees on me.

I asked him, "Will the kids show up for the transfer?"

"One of them will, the eldest will come sign."

I asked again, "Is there any problem with this place?"

Zhou smiled. "What problem? It's old, not a dangerous building. Title's clean. The old lady passed and nobody's lived there since, just gathered some dust."

I walked around the living room once more.

Only when I was about to leave did I notice a few details I hadn't caught before.

In the corner of the living room, a yellowed New Year painting of a chubby baby in a red belly band holding a big carp, pasted so long the paper had gone brittle, edges curling.

Under the coffee table glass, at the very bottom, a white porcelain bowl with half a bowl of rice. Three burned incense sticks standing upright in the rice, blackened, crooked, unclear what they were offering to.

And above the master bedroom door frame, a small red strip of paper, the characters so faded I had to lean in close to barely make them out—"Safe Coming and Going."

All things from when the old lady was alive. Normal. Old folks, they believe in this stuff.

I didn't think much of it.

Paid the money, signed the papers, got the key.

That afternoon when I walked out of the apartment, the sun was good, warm on my skin. I clutched that brass key, an old copper coin hanging from the keychain—I couldn't tell if the old lady left it or Zhou put it on there.

I pulled out my Nokia and called Dayong.

"Dayong, I bought a place."

"Huh? For real? Where?"

"Old complex north of the city, sixth floor, two bedrooms."

"How much?"

"Fifty-two thousand."

Silence on the other end for two seconds.

"Fifty-two thousand? Two bedrooms?" Dayong's voice changed. "You fucking kidding me? Something's wrong with that price, right?"

"What could be wrong? Old lady passed away, kids selling in a hurry."

"You're crazy. That price, something's absolutely wrong. You got scammed, didn't you?"

"Contract's signed, title's transferred, what problem could there be?"

"I can't put my finger on it…" Dayong clicked his tongue. "I just got a bad feeling. Fifty-two thousand, two bedrooms, that's too unreal."

"You're just jealous." I laughed and cursed him. "I finally got a place, and you're still renting."

Hung up, rode my bike back toward the company. Passed the vegetable market, bought a bunch of scallions, three yuan of meat, planning to cook myself a proper dinner that night.

The brass key dug into my thigh in my pocket.

I touched it and thought, alright, finally got my own nest.

Only later did I realize Dayong was dead right. I just didn't listen at the time.

And those details I didn't think much about—the covered wok, the bowl of rice and incense, the red strip above the door frame—they were always there, waiting for me to move in.
